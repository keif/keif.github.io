With all things, there's always caveats - and I feel like this is one that is often misunderstood.

People assume: "Choice one and choice two" instantly means "…and the opposite of choice three."

I feel like it drastically oversimplifies the purpose of the triangle. Like so much in technology, with its acronyms and principles and definitions - it always comes back to "context, nuance, and it depends."

> If a job’s worth doing, it’s worth doing well.  
>  – Proverb

> Never half-ass two things. Whole-ass one thing.
> – Ron Swanson

# Fast + Cheap = Bad Product

Makes sense, right? Two choices, means omitting the third. That's not the case, though. It means things like the _polish_, the _grit_, the focus on _quality_, _user-experience_ - the things that make a product "good" - get sacrificed. It's not a guarantee, **it depends on scope**, of course.

But if you want it fast and cheap? Don't be surprised it is rough around the edges.

# Good + Fast = Expensive

Again - team and organization dependent - this doesn't mean "you'll spend ten times the cost" just that the focus is on speed of delivery and quality, which often means extra man hours and crunch time. I've seen agencies use this as a model, and then burn out their workers because they never get a breather "because they're too valuable to deliver those projects that pay the bills." Your agency also gets talked about in the industry as a place to avoid, because the focus isn't on "healthy people" but on "making as much money and churning through developers."

# Good + Cheap = Slow

This is also a weird setup. Some people assume it means "they won't pay a lot, they'll get great work, but it'll take forever." Honestly, I feel like this is the best model you can hope for.

1. You're not overpaying.
2. Your workers are focused on delivering quality all the time
3. You're not attached to a deadline, you can make iterations and continuous delivery happen, and ensure quality going forward.

# The Best Teams

The best teams can do any of these things. But the best _management_ knows when their employees are getting burned out, and to not push aggressive "all three points" on every project. That's unsustainable for a long period of time - and it's why "work-life balance" became an important pillar in people's job offerings. They want to know they can take a personal moment and not deal with wrath from work because "they had a baby" or "they got married" or "their parent died and they need to take time off."

The Triangle is a guideline to set expectations. And anyone that's worked in enterprise has no doubt seen more than one instance of "we hired a vendor who is fast + cheap" and then your in-house team hates them, because they spend the next month(s)/year(s) fixing the bugs in the implementation. Or "this vendor we paid a lot of money for - that guarantees quality and speed" only to find out they sold you on their A-Team offering, but that is the team who gets called to look impressive - you get the C-Team, if you're lucky. But you'll be paying the same!
