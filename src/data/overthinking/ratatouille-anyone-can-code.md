I think the introduction of AI is changing how people work with code, and it's one of the true "disruptors" of anything that's come before it.

Intellisense was a game-changer. IDEs were a game-changer. I bet someone out there still argues that if you use anything other than VIM or emacs then "you're not a real developer."

Now I'm reading: "Using AI? You're not a real developer."

To misquote Ratatouille: anyone can code.

In Pixar's Ratatouille, the motto "Anyone can cook" means that while not every single person is destined to be a genius, true greatness can come from anyone, regardless of their background or social status.

AI removed a lot of barriers. A lot of people think of themselves as "programmers" or "developers" now, which irritates the gatekeepers who think "only those who followed my path are worthy." Anything else is "lesser."

And that's bullshit.

When I first got into programming, I had zero insight. We got online, I had no idea what to do. I was finding websites, but no details on how to create. I reached out to developers online at the time — never got a response, ever. I was clueless, bumbling around online. I discovered HTML and CSS on my own, trying to dissect how people made websites. To me, this was fun — I was figuring out the puzzle in reverse. Changing it. Making it better. And learning how to not make it worse.

Then, you start making your own puzzles. You're creating your own art. You're reverse engineering because you think their code is bad, or needs an improvement, or you need to adjust something for your use case. It's fun! You migrate things between languages, you discover the concepts behind the words people say. Currying? Obfuscation? OOP? You get it now!

After years of building and learning, along comes AI. Suddenly — that impostor syndrome you thought you had a handle on is back. It's needling you. Developers with next-to-no experience are generating huge PRs. Your anxiety kicks in. So many PRs. So much code. It's overwhelming! Now, everyone is a chef!

Except… you have experience. The kind that comes from poring over documentation, reverse engineering code, breaking production once and learning what you never want to do again. You're a god damned head of the kitchen. And if the trash boy alters your soup — you're going to want to know about it and fix it.

But you don't. Because you didn't see it. And you didn't see the rat fix it.

You just assumed "trash boy" did it all now, and you're struggling to understand — he's a trash boy with no experience, and he improved _your soup_? Skinner freaks out when he _hears_ that Linguini (without knowing about Remy) has altered _his_ soup. He tries it — it's delicious. But how can Linguini be BETTER than him?

![Linguini catches Remy in the act, realizing the rat has been the one cooking.](@/assets/images/blog/ratatouille-remy-discovered.gif)
_Image credit: Ratatouille / Pixar / Disney_

In the movie, we see the relationship between Linguini and Remy progress. Remy and Linguini learn to work together as a TEAM.

![Remy inside Linguini's shirt, comically flailing his arms and knocking over glasses as they figure out how to move as one.](@/assets/images/blog/ratatouille-spilling-wine.gif)
_Image credit: Ratatouille / Pixar / Disney_

But Linguini is blind to his companion. Remy is running the show. And as time goes on, Linguini learns how singular Remy is being. Only Remy has good ideas. Only Remy should add ingredients. Colette? With her years of experience? Clearly inferior.

![Remy hidden under Linguini's toque, pulling his hair like reins to steer him through the kitchen.](@/assets/images/blog/ratatouille-remy-guides-linguini.gif)
_Image credit: Ratatouille / Pixar / Disney_

But Linguini realizes it — and calls Remy out. Colette is a PROFESSIONAL. She knows what she is doing, too.

Colette is the piece the whole discourse forgets. She isn't the AI, and she isn't the newcomer. She's the senior chef who came up the hard way — the one Linguini needs to actually run a kitchen, even after Remy has taught him how to cook. When she gives Linguini his first real lesson, she isn't teaching him recipes; she's teaching him how a professional kitchen works. Knife discipline. Timing. Why you don't leave a bay leaf in the finished dish. That's the layer AI doesn't reach on its own. A model can produce the soup. Colette teaches you why it's _good_ soup, and how not to burn the restaurant down when you're plating fifty of them at once.

When she finally accepts Remy, she doesn't stop being Colette. She's still the professional. She's just made room for the rat too. Anyone can code — but not everyone will run the kitchen.

When Skinner discovers Remy, he tries to trap him, control him, and do everything he can to ruin Linguini and keep everything under his control — his predecessor's restaurant (mainly, his name) to cash out and make easy money making an inferior product. He LOVES Remy's output, but is too motivated by greed and jealousy to recognize what's in front of him.

![Skinner lunging to grab Linguini's toque, trying to expose the rat hidden underneath.](@/assets/images/blog/ratatouille-skinner-hat-grab.gif)
_Image credit: Ratatouille / Pixar / Disney_

And Ego? He knows good food. He knows REALLY good food.

Linguini learns to listen and respect Colette, even when Remy tries to override her. He's learned that there is something to experience — and that no one person owns the taste or the recipe. And as he owns up to the truth — Remy is the chef, not Linguini — his staff quits. Except Colette. She returns, and accepts Remy and his clan.

Skinner and Ego both taste Remy's cooking. The former angry to find something great coming from Linguini's kitchen; the latter recognizing the surprise of a real chef. It costs Ego his job, but then he goes into business with Remy, Colette, and Linguini, cooking together — he's happy, they're happy, and they're _cooking_.

> "In the past, I have made no secret of my disdain for Chef Gusteau's famous motto, 'Anyone can cook.' But I realize, only now do I truly understand what he meant. Not everyone can become a great artist; but a great artist _can_ come from _anywhere_."
>
> — Anton Ego
